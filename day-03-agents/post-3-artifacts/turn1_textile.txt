## How Budget 2026-27 Affects the Textile Industry

The Budget takes a broad, multi-pronged approach to textiles — combining a landmark new programme, infrastructure investment, export incentives, skilling, MSME support, and sustainability measures. Here is a sector-by-sector breakdown.

---

### 1. The Integrated Programme for Textiles (Core Policy — *"Textile, sports goods, and village industries"*)

The centrepiece is a new **Integrated Textile Programme** with five distinct sub-parts:

| Sub-scheme | What it does |
|---|---|
| **National Fibre Scheme** | Promotes self-reliance in natural fibres (silk, wool, jute), man-made fibres, and new-age fibres |
| **Textile Expansion and Employment Scheme** | Modernises traditional clusters via capital support for machinery, technology upgradation, and common testing & certification centres |
| **National Handloom and Handicraft Programme** | Consolidates existing schemes to deliver targeted support for weavers and artisans |
| **Tex-Eco Initiative** | Promotes globally competitive and *sustainable* textiles and apparels |
| **Samarth 2.0** | Upgrades the textile skilling ecosystem through collaboration with industry and academic institutions |

---

### 2. Mega Textile Parks

Beyond the Integrated Programme, the Budget proposes to set up **Mega Textile Parks in challenge mode**, with an explicit focus on **technical textiles** and value addition. This signals a push up the textile value chain, beyond conventional apparel/fabric manufacturing.

---

### 3. Mahatma Gandhi Gram Swaraj Initiative

A new **Mahatma Gandhi Gram Swaraj** initiative will strengthen **khadi, handloom, and handicrafts** with support for:
- Global market linkage and branding
- Training, skilling, and quality improvement
- Synergy with the **One-District-One-Product** initiative
- Benefits for weavers, village industries, and rural youth

---

### 4. Export Incentives — Customs/Indirect Tax Measures (*"Indirect Taxes: review of exemptions and export promotion"* + *Annexure*)

Two direct relief measures for textile exporters:

- **Extended re-export window:** The time period for export of the final product — using duty-free imported inputs — has been **extended from 6 months to 12 months** for exporters of **leather or textile garments**. This gives exporters significantly more operational flexibility.
- **Export promotion on inputs:** The existing duty-free import benefit on specified inputs (currently for leather/synthetic footwear) is being extended to **Shoe Uppers**, which is directly relevant to textile-linked accessories manufacturing.

These changes are confirmed in the Indirect Tax Annexure (Section D.2 and D.3).

---

### 5. MSME & Cluster Support — Indirect Benefit to Textile SMEs (*"Legacy industrial clusters and Champion MSMEs"*)

Textile manufacturing is heavily MSME-driven. The Budget offers three layers of support that directly benefit textile SMEs:

- **₹10,000 crore SME Growth Fund** — equity support to create "Champion" enterprises
- **₹2,000 crore top-up to the Self-Reliant India Fund** — sustains access to risk capital for micro enterprises
- **TReDS reforms** — over ₹7 lakh crore has already been unlocked via the Trade Receivables Discounting System; new measures mandate TReDS for CPSE purchases from MSMEs, add credit guarantee support, link GeM to TReDS, and enable TReDS receivables as asset-backed securities — all improving liquidity for textile MSEs.
- **Revival of 200 legacy industrial clusters** — many of India's legacy textile hubs (e.g., powerloom clusters) would qualify under this scheme, which targets infrastructure and technology upgradation.

---

### 6. E-Commerce Export Boost (*"Indirect Taxes: SEZs, customs process, ease of living"*)

The Budget proposes the **complete removal of the ₹10 lakh per consignment value cap on courier exports**. This is highly significant for small textile artisans, handloom weavers, and apparel start-ups trying to sell directly to global buyers via e-commerce platforms — a barrier that previously limited their export scale.

---

### 7. Sustainability Angle

The **Tex-Eco Initiative** signals that the Budget is aligning with global sustainability trends in textiles — likely covering eco-friendly production, certifications, and climate-compliant supply chains. This is relevant as major importing markets (EU, US) increasingly impose sustainability requirements on textile imports.

### Overall Assessment

This is arguably **one of the most comprehensive textile Budgets in recent years**. Rather than a single scheme, the government has architected an entire ecosystem — from raw fibre to skilled workers, from cluster infrastructure to global e-commerce exports, and from traditional handlooms to technical textiles. The sustainability and skilling components also suggest long-term structural reforms, not just short-term fiscal support. Notably, the Budget does **not explicitly state specific ₹ allocations** for the textile sub-schemes themselves (such as the Integrated Programme or Mega Textile Parks individually); those figures would be in the detailed expenditure budget documents rather than the speech.
